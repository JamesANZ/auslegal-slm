and Mr Frigger have applied to review a taxation of costs where their liability
for costs pursuant to court orders was taxed at
$160,098.41. They challenge
every item of the relevant bill of costs. They also seek to advance arguments
to the effect that the
lawyers involved were not entitled to indemnity in
respect of their costs.
18 October 2024, orders were made requiring Mrs and Mr Frigger to provide
security for costs of their review application in the
amount of $10,000:
Frigger v Banning (Application for Security for Costs on Review of
[2024] FCA 1207.
Since then, the review proceedings have been
stayed pending provision of that security.
regard to the failure to provide security, the respondents now seek orders to
the following effect:
(1) The review application is dismissed.
(2) Mrs and Mr Frigger do pay the costs of the review application to be assessed
on a lump sum basis.
(3) An amount of $60,000 provided pursuant to an order for security for costs
made in the proceedings, be paid out to the solicitors
for the respondents.
(4) Until further order, Mrs and Mr Frigger be required to obtain the leave of a
judge before filing any further applications in
the proceedings.
were made for the parties to file submissions as to whether the orders should be
made. Submissions have been filed by all
parties. As there are no factual

principles concerning
the exercise of the discretion as to costs which have been
addressed by written submissions, the determination of the matter would
significantly aided by an oral hearing. Accordingly, I deal with the issue of
costs on the papers.
written submissions from Mrs and Mr Frigger are in the following
The Applicants are not asking for the court to make a final decision about
liability of the cost claimant to pay legal cost (Indemnity
Principle).
The costs claimant rely for security for cost on the same evidence and
arrangement that they relied on for the original bill of costs.
When the court ordered the Friggers' security for costs, the court had
effectively already decided the main ground for review, that
arrangements do not contravene the indemnity principle.
In those circumstances, it is a pointless exercise to pay security for costs for
a review that has already been decided against the
Applicants.
The only right available to the Applicants is an appeal against the dismissal of
the review application.
and Mr Frigger offer no explanation for their delay in providing security. They
also make no submission as to why the amount

Mrs and Mr Frigger submit that their 'main ground for review' concerning whether
the indemnity principle has been met, has
effectively already been decided by
the Court. I do not understand the basis for that contention. In previous
decisions in the

acting as a taxing officer had authority to determine whether the indemnity
principle was satisfied:
Frigger v Banning (No 13)

There are grounds raised in the application now brought
to review the taxation which concern whether the indemnity principle has
met. They have not been determined. I observe that the taxation of costs is
not a rehearing and issues arise as to the extent
to which grounds can be
advanced on the review of a taxation in the form in which they have been
expressed by Mrs and Mr Frigger.
However, it is not the case that the matters
raised have been adjudicated. All that has been determined is that security
be provided before the review of the taxation proceeds.
