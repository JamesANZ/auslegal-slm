SUMMARY AND CONCLUSION
The central issue for determination by this Tribunal is whether the visa
applicant Marion Penn and review applicant Trevor Crowley
are able to satisfy
the substantive provisions of subregulation 1.03, which provides a definition
of the term
'special need relative'
. These grounds are relied upon by
the parties in this instance with a view to demonstrating satisfaction of the
criteria. In particular, given the said definition, it is
the mother of both parties, Mrs Ellen Crowley, aged 91 years, who requires
assistance in this instance.
The authorities in relation to
'special need relative'
instructive. The Tribunal has consistently followed the observations of
Burchett J in
Fuduche v Minister for Immigration, Local Government and
Ethnic Affairs
[1993] FCA 503
(1993) 117 ALR 418
, as follows:-
'It is appropriate to observe the definition of 'special need relative' is
largely concerned with medical issues. There must be
a need, not necessarily
permanent but at least long term, for some form of assistance because of death,
disability, prolonged illness
or other serious circumstances. (The last
matter, of course, need not be of medical, unless the earlier words create a
genus, which
would be a very restrictive view to take of a beneficial provision
under the broad rubric of 'special need', particularly as there
cannot be a
genus of medical conditions of the citizens of residents to be assisted, since
the first condition is death). There
must be an inability to obtain 'the
assistance' from the other sources mentioned ....
...The assistance with which the relation is concerned is the assistance the
relative is able to provide. If that is mowing lawns,
of course, other may do
it. The more complex and personal it is, the less this can be so. Indeed,
such cases are probably at the
heart of the benevolent intent of this
regulation which, on ordinary principles, as I have already indicated, should
be given a broad
and generous construction in the favour of the Australian
citizen and resident that it was intended to benefit ....
There is also another aspect of this consideration. The definition of 'special
need relative' does not ask only whether the particular
assistance the relative
can give is in fact replaceable. It also asks whether it can 'reasonably' be
obtained elsewhere. From that
point of view, no-one would suggest it
reasonable to expect a person suffering from psychiatric illness of the
severity here in question
to obtain the same support and encouragement from
uninvolved strangers (however professional) as that available from her loved
In respect of the matter which must also be considered by this Tribunal, namely
the reasonable availability of alternative sources
of support and care, the
Tribunal has held that
'reasonableness ... should have a subjective measure,
and what is reasonable in one case may not be reasonable in another. The
of the circumstances affecting the individual and his family must be
considered in each case ....'
Re Yung Hwagchi
(IRT Decision
2440, 9 August 1993)).
Further support for such an approach can also be found in the decision of
Foster J in
Moskal v Minister for Immigration, Local Government and Ethnic
(1994) 125 ALR 307
at 315 as follows:-
'Once the particular needs are carefully and accurately delineated by the
decision-maker, the next question must be whether these
needs generate 'a
permanent or long term need for assistance'. If this question be answered in
the affirmative, then the decision-maker
must determine, in a case where no
Australian citizen or permanent resident is available to provide such
assistance, whether they
can be provided by 'welfare, hospital, nursing or
community services in Australia' or can only be provided by a 'special need
This question again, cannot be properly addressed or answered by a mere general
and broad consideration of the types of services
available in Australia.
Common humanity demands that a disadvantaged citizen not be deprived of the
special caring assistance of
a near relative on the basis that, in some general
sense, it may be arguable that some aspect of that assistance can be provided
through visits from community welfare organisations. If there be demonstrated
a clear and definite need for ongoing emotional support,
attributable to the
special condition of the citizen, then the question is necessarily and squarely
raised, whether this support
can reasonably be provided by such organisations.
The essential issue for determination in such a case is whether such
organisations
can reasonably supply the necessary special care available from
the 'special need relative'.'

the Tribunal now sets outs its findings as follows.

The medical and other evidence before the Tribunal has confirmed, in the

has a long term need for assistance as a result of a prolonged illness and/or
other serious circumstances. On
the basis of the Tribunal's long term
treatment of these provisions, including the preferred meaning of the term

, and in the context of the aforementioned
Federal Court authorities, the Tribunal is prepared to find that the assistance
by the review applicant's mother cannot reasonably be obtained from
another relative, or more particularly, from an institution or
Australia. The Tribunal, closely following professional expert opinion, finds
that there is a lack of adequacy and appropriateness
in the institutional and
community services which are potentially available to the review applicant's
mother in this case. In the
Tribunal view, given Ellen Crowley's current
condition, it is best that she remains in her daughter's full time care in the
dignity. For this purpose, the Tribunal finds that Marion
Penn should remain
in Australia for such purpose.
Accordingly, the Tribunal finds that the visa applicant has satisfied the
definition of
'special need relative'
as set out in subregulation 1.03
in this instance. On such basis, the Tribunal is also satisfied that the
relevant prescribed criteria
