Issues for Determination
Three principal issues for determination were raised in respect of this
application. They could be summarised as:
* Whether the development could lead to an unacceptable loss of amenity for
neighbours due to noise and overlooking.
* Whether the traffic and parking likely to be generated would lead to traffic
hazards in Olive Grove.
* Whether the siting and design of this development was site responsive or
would detract from the character of the neighbourhood.
Each of these issues is discussed below.
The plans accompanying the application depict windows and balconies to both
sides of the proposed front wing. These balconies would
be raised between 2.8
and 3.8m above the undercroft or ground level car park and would have views
towards Mrs Venus' front dwelling
as well as the side walls and front yard of
the Saglietti's dwelling. In the case of Mrs Venus, the distance between her
and the face of the balconies is between 20 and 25m. In the case of
the Sagliettis the distance to their windows is approximately
In addition, the rear wings would extend to within 3 and 5m of the side
boundaries, with the setback developed as a landscaped path
system. On the
Saglietti's side the windows would only be located in those sections of the
building set back at least 5m.
The plans also depict the construction of a 1.8m high side and rear boundary
fences as well as a degree of excavation for the rear
parts of the rear wing -
the latter in order to maintain a more level internal layout.
In a 1997 decision of the former Administrative Appeals Tribunal and with
respect to a proposal to use this land for a child care

the noise of children playing could be detrimental to the amenity of the

the balconies in the front wing.
The Tribunal was provided with no evidence to suggest that special

The construction of a 1.8m high fence around the rear wings would prevent

2. Traffic Safety
In the case of
N.F. Clarke v City of Knox and Others
the Tribunal found
that the use of this land for a child care centre should not be allowed due
primarily to the hazard of vehicles
entering and exiting the site near the
crest and at times when the angle of the sun can be blinding for persons using
Olive Grove.
This concern was again raised in the subject proceeding. In this
instance the driveway crossover is to be moved such that it is
now 80m west of
the crest. It was Mr Fairlie's evidence that this is now well in excess of the
55m stopping distance required for
vehicles travelling at 60km/h. He noted
also how, compared to a child care centre where peak parent arrivals and
departure times
may coincide, at certain times of the year with the rising or
setting of the sun, this is not a peak time for vehicles associated
subject use.
Mr Fairlie's evidence was persuasive also in relation to the volume of cars and
other vehicles likely to be generated by this use.
Due to the relatively low
staff to resident ratios in special accommodation houses (compared with nursing
following surveys of similar facilities, it was his
opinion that the likely peak parking demand for staff would be for six spaces
and for visitors, 11. This can be provided without recourse to kerbside
parking. He suggested also that the likely number of trips
generated by the
use per day would be in the order of 40 to 60, ie. between 20 and 30 in and 20
to 30 out. This would have very
little impact on perceived traffic flows in
Olive Grove.
3. Is the Design and Siting of the Development Site Responsive?
It was contended that the topographical constraints of this site , coupled with
the requirement for such uses to be all on the one
level, had resulted in a
development which was more bulky and visually dominant than would be case for a
use with less stringent
levels requirements. Mr Atkins observed also that, in
an attempt to address the issue of bulk, a circuitous network of internal
had had to be devised. He questioned the appropriateness of such a movement
In giving evidence Mr Perch pointed to other special accommodation house
developments where internal ramping had been successfully
applied, noting also
that at 1 in 14 the gradients of the proposed ramps were very shallow. He
acknowledged however that it was
not usual to apply such an extensive ramping
system. Mr Perch advised also that the internal layout of such facilities was
by other agencies and the Tribunal acknowledges this fact.
The topography of this site is however a constraint for a use that needs to be
on the one level. In the subject instance this has
led to the use of a two
storey wing projecting towards the street and a single level rear extension
which is partly excavated into
the rear boundary. It is apparent however that
in the subject instance this possible constraint has certain positive
attributes.
It has for example enabled the car parking area to be located
below the accommodation wing, with little need for excavation and
added benefit of maximising ground level landscaping rather than having this
filled by an open lot car park. On one reading,
this is a site responsive
response. An issue for determination is whether, by meeting this constraint
using a design which incorporates
a forward projecting two storey wing, and
rear excavation, will integrate well with the neighbourhood and not detract
from the character
of the area. I regard the following to be the relevant
objectives and criteria of the Guide in relation to these issues:
Neighbourhood Character
E3.O1 To achieve medium density development which is respectful of its
neighbourhood.
E3.O3 To maintain the garden character of neighbourhoods.
E3.C1 Medium density development among established housing should be
designed to respect the character of the neighbourhood (in design
built form, look for characteristics of; massing proportion, roof form and
pitch, fa√ßade articulation and detailing,
window and door proportions,
verandahs, eaves and parapets, building materials, patterns, textures and
colours, building setbacks).
E3.C2 New developments should respect and maintain the garden or landscape
character of an area where this is a dominant feature of
neighbourhood.
E3.C3 New development should provide for the retention or planting of trees
with spreading crowns.
Site Layout and Landscaping
E4.O1 To integrate the layout of developments with the neighbourhood and
abutting use and development.
E4.C1 Developments should integrate well with the neighbourhood. Enhance
existing streets, maintain amenity for adjacent residents
E4.C2 Layouts should respond positively to site features and overcome site
constraints."
It was Ms Garnett's evidence that the extent of supplementary
landscaping, taken with the existing boundary planting will ensure that
proposed development, when viewed from any side, would not be visually
intrusive or alien to the neighbourhood notwithstanding
its comparatively
greater visual bulk.
Mr Underwood raised the very relevant issue also that such uses must have a
place somewhere.
Arguing against Mr Bastone's submission that the appropriate
location for these uses is within or adjacent to a commercial precinct,
Underwood suggested that a residential location was to be preferred. He
contended that in the subject locality, the topography
and landscaping could be
employed as an aid to conceal the necessarily greater bulk which such a
facility presents. I accept these
submissions. In another special
accommodation case which came before me,
Currawong Lodge v City of
Manningham and Others
(Appeal No. 97/16056 referred to AATR editorial
comment page 2) I found, in a newly developing part of Donvale where the
was very open and undulating, that the visual prominence and bulk of
a 37 bed special accommodation house would be too much at odds
character of the area, notwithstanding that certain of the new dwellings were
of some size. I also found the location to
be too isolated from public
transport and services. The subject site is not so isolated. It is also
within a well established and
well vegetated residential neighbourhood and I am
satisfied that the necessary bulk of a special accommodation house has greater
potential for integration into such a setting.
Such integration cannot however rely on vegetation alone. As noted in the
criteria of Elements 3 and 4 of the Guide, which consider
neighbourhood character and site layout and landscaping, it is appropriate
take certain cues from other elements within the neighbourhood, including built
form elements. While I am satisfied that the
two storey bulk of the building
projecting forwards towards the street can, to some extent, be ameliorated by
the existing and proposed
vegetation, I find that a more sensitive
architectural treatment for this forward projecting wing is necessary in order
that it does not read as an alien structure in this setting.
In his written statement of evidence, Mr Perch made the following statement:
"The external character of the building is subject to more advanced and
detailed studies of preferred ultimate design expression which
will take place
prior to application for building permits. This presentation is schematic only
and begs to assume an attractive
roof design and wall treatment resolution when
more definitive design parameters are to be studied and confirmed."
As I suggested in the course of the hearing, I find this to be a rather
enigmatic statement. It suggests that the final appearance
of the building,
including the roof treatment and walls - both of which are fundamental
components - could be determined
any planning permit had issued.
Given the matters required to be taken into account when considering such an
application - including
the test of whether the development has been designed
to respect the character of the neighbourhood - I regard deferral of such
details until
a permit issued as an incorrect approach.
Having made a detailed inspection of the area I find that the parapeted and
somewhat terraced
style of the proposed development - as depicted on Mr Perch's
plans - bears no relationship with its Olive Grove neighbours. However,
little difficulty a skilled designer could, I am confident, devise a more
sympathetic treatment including a remodelling of the
front elevation to reduce
the visual impact of the garage entry and to engender a more domestic
appearance. The selection of a tiled
roof form and use of a recessive brick
colour, reflective of prevailing materials within the neighbourhood, would also
ensure a more
harmonious result.
The issue of neighbourhood character is not limited to the streetscape impact
of a development. As was noted
in the December 1994 report of the Vic Code 2
Review Panel at page 27:
"Character is the inter-relationship of various aspects of built form,
topography, vegetation, density, subdivision pattern and activity
both in the
public and private domain. Streetscape is one aspect of such character ...
character is also used to refer to the "feel"
of an area - the atmosphere
perceived not only from the street but from the backyard. This is a much more
amorphous concept, reliant
as much on intangible qualities such as "peaceful",
"secluded", "quiet", "friendly" etc, as on descriptions such as "green and
Most medium density developments, particularly on standard infill sites,
borrow their amenity from neighbouring properties. If these
are redeveloped
also, the cumulative impact of the loss of trees becomes obvious and begins to
significantly change the "character"
of the area ...
One of the hallmarks of Melbourne is its gardens and the predominance of
trees across the majority of its suburbs. It is the single
feature common to
most peoples perception of the character of their neighbourhood. It is the
loss of their softening effect and
the privacy they afford which is most keenly
felt when adjoining land is developed for units. Even where suitable design
are employed to prevent overlooking, buildings are still perceived as
intrusive without a buffer of vegetation.
Therefore, if change is to occur within the suburbs, it needs to do so in a
context which respects and maintains the essential garden
character of
Melbourne where gardens are a dominant feature of the "character" or "feel" of
a neighbourhood."
In the subject instance the presence of substantial trees on the site and on
neighbouring sites is an important feature of the neighbourhood.
rising topography, the trees in the rear gardens of dwellings on this northern
side of Olive Grove provide a well vegetated
backdrop to the neighbourhood.
The conifers on the rear boundary, most of which are within the car park of the
adjoining nursing
development would remove a majority of the trees in the rear yard and the
wing would be constructed to within approximately 4m of the trunks of the
conifers and a mature elm (inaccurately labelled as
a pine on Ms Garnett's
planting plan). Furthermore, whilst the plans indicate the retention of a
centrally located, semi-mature
Blue Spruce, Ms Garnett could not be clear about
the possible impacts on this tree of three walls to the rear wing which are to
within approximately 3m of its trunk.
I have noted already the absence of a site context plan showing the precise
location of trees
and other features on neighbouring sites. I could not be
satisfied from Ms Garnett's evidence, especially in view of the excavation
proposed for the rear boundaries, that the health of this Blue Spruce and of
nearby significant trees is assured. I find therefore
that a more detailed
appraisal of the impacts of the rear wings is required to be carried out. This
may well indicate that these
wings must be pulled further off the rear
boundary. This being the case the greater setbacks could be developed with
further gardens
and, more particularly, a range of new canopy trees which in
time could make their own contribution to the backdrop planting for
neighbourhood.
On the basis of the plans as presently submitted
it would have been the finding of this Tribunal that no permit should issue.
in view of the nature of my conclusions - which are essentially that
is acceptable provided the built form can be made more
respectful of its neighbours and the significant vegetation to the rear, I
think it appropriate in this instance to direct the preparation and circulation
of amended plans which address these concerns.
1. The applicant is directed to prepare amended plans which:
(a) Detail measures to be taken to screen views between west-facing windows and
balconies on the front wing and habitable room windows
in the dwelling at No.
11 Olive Grove. Such measures to include an analysis of levels and distances
to windows in No. 11.
(b) Depict an architectural style more responsive to elements within the
neighbourhood, including a reappraisal of the front elevation
to reduce the
visual dominance of the garage entry and to engender a more domestic
appearance.
(c) Give consideration to the likely impact of the rear wings and associated
excavation on the health of the Blue Spruce and significant
rear boundary trees
- both within and external to the site - including conifers and an elm. The
report of a professional arborist
must be obtained and must set out
recommendations in relation to appropriate setbacks, excavation and footing
designs. Such recommendations
to be depicted, where appropriate, on the plans
or in an accompanying schedule.
2. Upon preparation, the amended plans and associated reports and schedules
must be circulated for comment to all other parties and
a copy forwarded at the
same time to the Tribunal. If parties wish to make comment these must be
forwarded in writing to the Tribunal
within 14 days of receipt of the plans
etc. A copy must also be forwarded at the same time to the permit applicant.
The Tribunal
will then proceed to determine the matter.
