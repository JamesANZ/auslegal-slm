DISCONTINUANCE OF WEEKLY PAYMENTS
Details: Factory hand with accepted compensable "trigger finger" disabilities
to both hands undergoing operation resulting in period
of incapacity for work -
additional concurrent disabilities to back and shoulders initially rejected by
exempt employer, but found
compensable and with resultant ongoing incapacity by
another Review Officer - Section 36 notices given to worker seeking to
discontinue
weekly payments - oral evidence taken from worker and a number of
medical experts and medical reports provided - view conducted
of work place -
worker found to have degenerative conditions to back and shoulders - worker
found to still suffer disabling effects
produced by his compensable disability
resulting in significant, but partial ongoing incapacity for work.

However, the general rule is that
a compensation authority must take its worker as it finds him or her and where a
previously asymptomatic
condition is made symptomatic by a work injury, the
resulting incapacity is considered fully compensable unless the contribution
non-work factors go so far as to make it impossible to find that the disability
comes within
at all. As Latham CJ said in
Corrimal-Balgownie Collieries
(1938) 61 CLR 120
[1938] HCA 70
"In determining whether incapacity results from an injury the law necessarily
adopts an idea of causation which, in a sense, isolates
the injury as a
causative element from other elements which are taken for granted or ignored.
If the addition of the injury to other
concurrently existing facts brings about
the incapacity, then the incapacity is regarded as resulting from the injury,
although in
fact it results from the injury taken together with the other
circumstances."
This citation was quoted with approval in
WorkCover v. Sainsbury
1993 5 WCATR 261
[1993] SAWCAT 49
where the Workers Compensation Appeal Tribunal expressed itself as
"The essential factor is whether the compensable disability is producing
disabling effects which reduce the worker's ability to sell
his labour. Such
disabling effects do not have to be the sole cause of the worker's reduced
ability to sell his labour, it is sufficient
if those disabling effects produced
by the compensable disability are a contributing cause of the partial
incapacity."
